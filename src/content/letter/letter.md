closing: Always your son,

Mom,

You were hard on us. I want to start there because I want to be
honest. There were nights I went to bed angry. There were standards
I couldn't meet, expectations I didn't understand, and rules I
thought were unfair right up until the moment I needed them.

What I didn't know then, and what nobody tells you when you're a
kid, is that the parents who hold the line are doing the harder
job. It would have been easier to let things slide. Easier to sign
the report card. Easier to skip church. Easier to let us off the
hook. You never did. The spine I have now, the work ethic, the
refusal to settle, the quiet way I still make my bed every morning
without thinking about it. All of that came from you.

You raised us in a house where God was real, where Sunday meant
something, where pride in our work was the rule, not the goal. You
taught us to give our best every single time, and you never
accepted less. Somehow, against all the rolled eyes and the doors
we weren't allowed to slam, the lesson took. Look at us now. Look
at who we became. That's you.

And then you became a grandmother, and that's the part I think
about most. The world got to see a side of you we always knew was
there but rarely saw in full. Bright. Cheerful. Patient beyond
measure. The first one to laugh, the last one to leave, the person
every grandchild runs to when they have something to show. You
became their soft place to land, the same way you'd been our hard
place to grow, and I love you for both.

You are my number one supporter. You always have my back. You
always will. And when I am old, and I am quiet, and I am the steady
one in some other family's kitchen humming a hymn nobody asked me
to hum, I will know exactly where I learned it.

I love you, Mom. Thank you for everything.
